American Homework

April 4, 2026 - 5 min read

It’s September, and I have to contextualize what events led to this call for war. It made us famous, and yet. The photographs don’t fade, but I can’t read their faces. They tell us we are weak, that together we could cobble together something like a formidable adversary, if we could only do the just, honourable deed they call surviving. Will we feel like it next year, next week? There’s no disarming anymore; we leave a guard in each room, and though they suffer our irresolution, we gather strength for opposition. We come back, or we don’t at all. Are there any other means of effectual resistance? We doubt it. Calling it the phantom of hope is too obvious, Mr. Alexander. We are not weak of means nor spirit, just spirit, and means. Is there any question of what the enemy could send against us, then? We are what they will. We are the enemy, so we are as invincible as we are. It’s good for us, this country we’ve made in our homes, fenced in by the walls. We fight our battles together, against, alone, as one. We were only raised for the fight; it does no good to point out the solitude, if it’s there at all. It’s far too late to retire from this contest anyway. We’ve only lived in submission. Let it come, the inevitable, the war. We live in its theatre. We can feel the earth shake with our feet off the ground. I repeat it, sir. Let it come. We are already in the field. Why stand idle? We paid the price here already. We know where we stand. We know when the soil will still.
